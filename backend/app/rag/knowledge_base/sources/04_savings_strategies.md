# Savings Strategies

## Pay Yourself First

The most effective savings habit: transfer money to savings on payday, before discretionary spending.

If you wait to save what is left at month-end, savings often become inconsistent.

**How to do it:**
1. Open a separate savings account
2. Set a standing order for payday
3. Start small (even £20-£50/month)
4. Increase amount when income rises

## UK Savings Accounts

### Cash ISA
- Tax-free interest up to annual ISA allowance (£20,000/year)
- Easy-access or fixed-term variants
- Useful for short/medium-term goals

### Lifetime ISA (LISA)
- For ages 18-39
- 25% government bonus on up to £4,000/year
- Use cases: first home or retirement (age 60+)
- Early withdrawal penalty applies for non-qualifying withdrawals

### Easy-Access Savings
- Instant liquidity
- Usually lower rate than fixed-term
- Best for emergency fund and short-term goals

### Fixed-Rate Savings
- Higher rate for locking funds (often 1-5 years)
- Reduced flexibility
- Best when timeline is known and liquidity need is low

## Build a Tiered Savings System

A practical structure:
1. **Tier 1** mini-buffer: £500-£1,000
2. **Tier 2** emergency fund: 3-6 months essentials
3. **Tier 3** goal pots: named targets (deposit, travel, etc.)
4. **Tier 4** long-term investing contributions

## Savings Goals Framework

For each goal define:
- what
- target amount
- deadline
- monthly contribution needed

Formula:
`required monthly saving = (target - current) / months remaining`

## Boosting Savings Rate

**Income-side actions:**
- negotiate pay rise
- overtime / side income
- sell unused assets

**Expense-side actions:**
- subscription audits
- renew/switch household contracts
- meal planning and waste reduction
- cap discretionary categories

## Compound Interest

Starting earlier can outweigh contributing larger amounts later.

Example concept:
- same monthly amount invested for longer tends to produce materially higher end value due to compounding duration.
