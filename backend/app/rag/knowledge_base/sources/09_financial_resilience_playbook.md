# Financial Resilience Playbook

## Core Objective

Financial resilience means maintaining control during income shocks, expense shocks, and behavioral drift.

A practical playbook should answer:
- How quickly do I run out of buffer under stress?
- Which expense categories create fragility?
- What sequence of actions most improves survival and recovery?

## Step 1: Build Baseline Visibility

Track these baseline metrics monthly:
- take-home income
- essential expenses
- discretionary expenses
- debt repayments
- savings buffer
- savings rate

Without baseline data, scenario outputs are noisy and hard to trust.

## Step 2: Create Tiered Expense Cuts

Prepare expense controls in advance:

### Tier A (immediate, low pain)
- pause unused subscriptions
- reduce impulse/entertainment spend
- switch variable bills where possible

### Tier B (moderate impact)
- reduce dining out frequency
- lower non-essential transport costs
- renegotiate phone/internet plans

### Tier C (high impact)
- housing adjustments
- vehicle changes
- debt restructuring plans

## Step 3: Emergency Buffer Targets

Recommended operating targets:
- initial safety floor: 1 month essentials
- short-term resilience: 3 months essentials
- robust resilience: 6 months essentials

If income is unstable or dependents are present, bias toward upper range.

## Step 4: Debt Risk Reduction

High-interest debt amplifies fragility by reducing monthly flexibility.

Priority order:
1. maintain emergency mini-buffer
2. attack highest APR debt
3. avoid new expensive debt during stress periods

## Step 5: Income Defense and Diversification

Resilience improves materially when you reduce dependence on a single income source.

Examples:
- increase skill portability
- maintain updated CV/network pipeline
- add low-volatility side income where feasible

## Step 6: Scenario Review Cadence

Re-run scenario tests when:
- income changes
- recurring expenses change significantly
- major debt milestones are reached
- every 3 to 6 months as routine

## Decision Framework After a Weak Score

If job-loss score is weak:
- prioritize buffer + fixed-cost reductions first

If emergency score is weak:
- increase liquid savings and reduce discretionary leakage

If promotion score gains are weak:
- lifestyle inflation is likely absorbing upside; enforce surplus automation

## Rule of Thumb

Resilience improves fastest when actions increase **monthly surplus** and **ending buffer** at the same time.
