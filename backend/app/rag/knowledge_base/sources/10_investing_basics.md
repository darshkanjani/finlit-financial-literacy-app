# Investing Basics (UK-Focused)

## Important Scope

This guide is educational only. It is not a personal recommendation to buy, sell, or hold any investment.

## Why People Invest

People invest to grow wealth over medium and long timeframes, often for:
- retirement
- house deposit goals with long horizons
- long-term financial independence

Investing usually involves short-term volatility in exchange for expected long-term growth.

## Key Principle: Time Horizon Matters

A practical rule:
- money needed in under 3 years: usually keep mostly in cash/easy-access products
- 3-7 years: mixed approach can be considered
- 7+ years: investments are often more suitable than cash alone

Short horizons are vulnerable to market drawdowns at the wrong time.

## Risk and Return

Higher expected return usually comes with higher risk.

Typical risk ordering (lower -> higher):
1. cash savings
2. government and high-grade bonds
3. diversified equity funds
4. concentrated single stocks / speculative assets

Risk is not just "price moves". It also includes:
- inflation risk (cash losing purchasing power)
- concentration risk (too much in one asset)
- behavior risk (panic selling)

## Diversification

Diversification means spreading exposure across many assets so one failure has limited impact.

Examples:
- global equity index fund instead of one company
- mixed stock/bond allocation
- avoiding oversized positions in a single sector

Diversification does not remove risk, but it can reduce avoidable risk.

## Asset Allocation (Simple Framework)

Asset allocation = split between growth assets (equities) and stabilizers (bonds/cash).

Example profiles (illustrative only):
- cautious: 30% equity / 70% bonds-cash
- balanced: 60% equity / 40% bonds-cash
- growth: 80% equity / 20% bonds-cash

Allocation should match:
- your time horizon
- ability to tolerate losses
- need for liquidity

## Costs and Fees Matter

Over long periods, costs compound against returns.

Track:
- platform fee
- fund OCF/expense ratio
- transaction fees
- FX fees (for non-GBP assets)

Lower costs improve net outcomes when strategy quality is similar.

## Tax Wrappers in the UK

Common wrappers:
- **Stocks & Shares ISA**: tax-efficient investing up to ISA allowance
- **Pension (SIPP/workplace)**: strong long-term tax advantages; access restrictions apply
- **General Investment Account (GIA)**: taxable, used after wrapper allowances

Tax rules can change, so check current HMRC guidance.

## Pounds-Cost Averaging (Regular Investing)

Investing a fixed amount regularly can:
- reduce timing pressure
- build consistency
- smooth emotional decision-making

It does not guarantee gains, but often improves discipline.

## Rebalancing

Portfolio weights drift over time (for example equities rally and become overweight).

Rebalancing means resetting to target allocation periodically (for example every 6-12 months or at threshold bands).

Benefits:
- maintains intended risk profile
- enforces disciplined buy/sell behavior

## Behavioral Mistakes to Avoid

Common mistakes:
- chasing recent winners
- panic selling during drawdowns
- checking portfolio too frequently
- changing strategy after every headline

A written plan (allocation, cadence, rules) reduces these errors.

## Practical Starter Checklist

1. Define goal and time horizon.
2. Build emergency fund before aggressive investing.
3. Pay off very high-interest debt first.
4. Choose a simple diversified allocation.
5. Use tax-efficient wrappers where possible.
6. Automate monthly contributions.
7. Rebalance on a schedule, not on emotion.
