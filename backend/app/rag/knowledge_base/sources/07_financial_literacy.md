# Financial Literacy Glossary and Key Concepts

## Core Terms

**Income** - Money coming in. This includes salary, wages, freelance earnings, benefits, rental income, or any other regular source of money.

**Expenses** - Money going out. Everything you spend money on - rent, food, transport, entertainment, subscriptions, etc.

**Budget** - A plan that shows how much money you have coming in and where it goes.

**Savings rate** - The percentage of your income you save rather than spend. Higher is better.

**Net worth** - Everything you own minus everything you owe. Positive net worth means you own more than you owe.

**Cash flow** - The movement of money in and out. Positive cash flow means more comes in than goes out.

## Interest

**Interest** - The cost of borrowing money, or the reward for saving it.

**APR (Annual Percentage Rate)** - The yearly cost of borrowing, including fees. Higher APR = more expensive debt. Used for credit cards, loans, overdrafts.

**AER (Annual Equivalent Rate)** - The yearly return on savings. Higher AER = better savings rate.

**Compound interest** - Interest earned on your original savings AND on the interest already earned. Over time, this creates exponential growth. Also works against you on debt - debt compounds too.

## Debt Terms

**Credit score** - A rating that tells lenders how reliably you repay debt. Higher scores get better interest rates. Affected by payment history, amounts owed, length of credit history.

**Minimum payment** - The smallest amount you can pay on a credit card or loan each month. Paying only the minimum is very expensive over time due to interest.

**Balance transfer** - Moving debt from one credit card to another, often to get a lower interest rate (or 0% for a period).

**Overdraft** - Spending more than you have in your bank account. Arranged overdrafts have agreed limits; unarranged overdrafts have high fees.

## Savings Terms

**ISA (Individual Savings Account)** - A tax-free savings or investment account. You don't pay tax on interest or gains inside an ISA.

**Easy-access account** - A savings account you can withdraw from at any time, usually with lower interest.

**Fixed-rate account** - A savings account where you agree to lock money away for a set time in exchange for higher interest.

**Standing order** - A regular automatic payment you set up from your bank account. Useful for automating savings.

## Investing Basics (Simple Overview)

**Stocks** - A share of ownership in a company. Value goes up and down. Higher risk, higher potential return over long periods.

**Bonds** - A loan to a company or government that pays you interest. Lower risk than stocks, lower return.

**Fund** - A pooled collection of investments (stocks, bonds, or both). Spreads risk across many companies.

**Pension** - A long-term savings plan for retirement. UK employees get employer contributions too. Very tax-efficient.

## Technical Terms Used In FinLit

**Stress scenario** - A modelled financial condition (job loss, emergency cost, promotion) used to simulate month-by-month outcomes.

**Resilience score (0-10)** - Composite stress score built from survival, end-buffer strength, cashflow health, and stability.

**RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)** - AI generation approach where relevant knowledge snippets are retrieved first and passed as context.

**FAISS** - A vector similarity index used to retrieve semantically relevant knowledge chunks for RAG.

**ML cutback estimate** - In job-loss mode, a machine-learning estimate of realistic spending reduction when a manual cutback value is not provided.

## Financial Wellbeing

Financial wellbeing means:
- Being able to cover everyday expenses
- Having a buffer for unexpected costs
- Being on track for future goals
- Not being excessively stressed about money

It is not about being rich - it is about having control and security.
